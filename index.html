The one thing

How to read a Korean skin clinic

In Korea, any licensed doctor can open a "skin clinic." Only about 2% are board-certified dermatologists.

The rest are run by GPs, family-medicine doctors, even OB-GYNs operating laser machines. That's not automatically bad — plenty are fine for a basic aqua peel. But for anything that needs real judgment (scars, pigmentation, lasers on your face), you want the credential.

Look for 피부과 전문의 — "board-certified dermatologist." It's on the clinic's Korean site and the doctor's wall. If a clinic won't tell you the doctor's specialty, that's your answer.

Doctor-led boutique

  • 1:1 time with the actual doctor
  • They may talk you out of a treatment
  • Private rooms, customized plan
  • Right for scars, pigment, anything invasive
  • Costs more — worth it for judgment calls

"Factory" clinic

  • Counselor sells before you see a doctor
  • Shared rooms, self-cleansing, conveyor pace
  • You pick off a menu vs. getting a diagnosis
  • Watch for non-refundable deposits + upsell
  • Fine for one simple, known want
Foreigner-pricing tip: screenshot prices from the UNNI app before you go — tourist quotes can run 2–3× local. A real clinic will put the dose (cc/mL) in writing. A suspiciously low headline price usually means a smaller dose.

Be careful

What we took off the list

Earlier drafts of this guide included these. Research pulled them — that's the whole point of a verified list.

  • PPEUM — documented scam-pattern complaints: surprise $3K bills, treatments invented on the spot, cards charged before an itemized total, and reports of gifts traded for 5-star reviews (so its high rating isn't trustworthy). Hard no.
  • Lamiche — a high-volume "factory" doing ~2,500 treatments/month in Jamsil (not Apgujeong, as it's often listed), with recurring complaints of rushed consults and surprise add-on costs.
  • "Maison," "Lily," "Tune & Tone" — names that float around English listicles but resolve to content-farm phantoms or nothing at all. Don't chase them.
  • Any clinic that won't name the doctor's specialty — and remember, a "skin clinic" run by a family-medicine doctor is legal but not the same as a 피부과 전문의.

Logistics

How to actually book

📲

UNNI (Global) — the foreigner app

The one booking app foreigners can actually use (Apple/Google login, no Korean ID). 40–70% off Western prices. Catch: "event prices" are loss-leaders, expect +20–30% if you decline the photo review.

🚫

Gangnam Unni — skip it

The domestic version is locked behind a Korean phone number + ID. You won't be able to use it, despite every listicle recommending it.

💬

KakaoTalk — set it up first

This is how clinics actually communicate. Install it and make an account before you fly; many of the best clinics (Ocean included) book this way.

🎫

Creatrip — verify the window

Gives 10% back as points (not cash) + English support + clinic freebies. The cashback window was only confirmed through end of June — re-check before you rely on it for a July trip.

Seoul 101

First time in Korea — the practical stuff

Not "visit a palace!" tips. Things that save you time and confusion in the first 48 hours.

🗺️

Download Naver Maps

Not Google Maps — it lags badly on Korean addresses. Naver Maps walks you to the exact building and floor. Grab it before you land.

🚇

T-money card at the airport

Any CU or 7-Eleven in arrivals. Works on all subways + buses. COEX = Samseong Station, Line 2 (green), Exit 5–6.

🚖

Kakao T for taxis

The Uber equivalent. Drivers rarely speak English — show the destination in Korean (grab it off the clinic's site).

🌡️

July is brutal

31°C, 85% humidity. Get a handheld fan from Daiso day one (₩2–5k). COEX AC is arctic inside — bring a layer.

💳

Cards mostly work

Apple Pay almost everywhere now. Carry ₩50–100k cash for small spots. No tipping, anywhere — it's genuinely not the culture.

🚪

Exit numbers matter

A station can have 15+ exits over several blocks. Wrong exit = a 15-min walk in the heat. Check the exit on Naver before you surface.

✈️

Before you leave: K-ETA

US passport holders need a K-ETA (travel authorization) — $10, online at k-eta.go.kr, up to 72h to process, valid 2 years. Not a visa, but you can't board without it. Apply a week out.

Useful at the clinic: screenshot 저는 민감성 피부예요 ("I have sensitive skin") and 처음이에요 ("this is my first time"). Every clinic will appreciate the effort and it sets the right expectations.
